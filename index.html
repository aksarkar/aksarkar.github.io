Research

My main research interests are in computational biology. I am particularly interested in developing novel algorithms and software tools to answer biological questions.

Graduate

I currently work in the Computational Biology group with Manolis Kellis at the Computer Science and Artificial Intelligence Lab.

My current work is on understanding how genotype contributes to human disease. I have developed statistical methods to interpret genome-wide association studies which do not reduce the number of markers considered using a p-value threshold. By doing so, we gain the power to detect many more common variants which each contribute small effect sizes towards the disease phenotype.

Using epigenetic annotations from the ENCODE and Roadmap Epigenomics consortia), we have identified cell-type–specific enhancers which show systematic bias in p-values for association with Type 1 Diabetes. Moreover, the methods suggest an unprecedented number (thousands) of variants are linked with this disease.

Undergraduate

I worked in the UNC Computational Genetics Research Group with Wei Wang.

The broad focus of my work was on genetic recombination in model organism populations. I collaborated on the implementation of haplotype and ancestry inference tools which infer alleles from genotypes and inheritance of contiguous regions of the genome. In particular, I worked on a dynamic programming–based tool and an HMM-based tool for ancestry inference.
